Normal Operators (N†N=NN†)\left(N^\dag N=NN^\dag\right)(N†N=NN†)

The most important property is that they have Spectral Decomposition:

  • Eigenvectors for different eigenvalues are orthogonal. (proof)
  • Eigenvectors form an orthonormal basis. So NNN can be written as: (proof)
    N=∑kλk∣ek⟩ ⁣ ⁣⟨ek∣ where ⟨ek∣el⟩=δklN=\sum_k \lambda_k \ket{e_k}\!\!\bra{e_k} \text{~where~}\braket{e_k|e_l}=\delta_{kl}N=k∑​λk​∣ek​⟩⟨ek​∣ where ⟨ek​∣el​⟩=δkl​

Some other properties:

  • Eigenvalues could be real or complex.
  • For any state ∣ψ⟩\ket \psi∣ψ⟩, we have: ∥N ⁣∣ψ⟩∥=∥N† ⁣∣ψ⟩∥\big\lVert{N\!\ket \psi}\big\rVert = \big\lVert{N^\dag\!\ket\psi}\big\rVert∥∥​N∣ψ⟩∥∥​=∥∥​N†∣ψ⟩∥∥​. (proof)
  • N†N^\dagN† is also a normal operator. And the eigenvectors are same as NNN, but with eigenvalues conjugate of NNN's. (proof)
    N† ⁣∣e⟩=λ∗∣e⟩whereN ⁣∣e⟩=λ∣e⟩N^\dag\!\ket e=\lambda^*\ket e\text{\quad where\quad}N\!\ket e = \lambda\ket eN†∣e⟩=λ∗∣e⟩whereN∣e⟩=λ∣e⟩
  • Can be separated into Hermitian and anti-Hermitian parts (N=A+iB)(N=A+iB)(N=A+iB) where they commute, AB=BAAB=BAAB=BA. (proof)
  • NNN is unitarily diagonalizable. The eigenvalues are the entries of the diagonal matrix and corresponding eigenvectors make the columns of the unitary matrix.

Unitary Operators (U†=U−1)\left(U^\dag = U^{-1}\right)(U†=U−1)

  • UUU is also normal because U†U=I=UU†U^\dag U = I = U U^\dagU†U=I=UU†. So spectral decomposition also applies here: (proof)
    U=∑kλk∣ek⟩ ⁣ ⁣⟨ek∣ where ⟨ek∣el⟩=δklU=\sum_k \lambda_k \ket{e_k}\!\!\bra{e_k} \text{~where~}\braket{e_k|e_l}=\delta_{kl}U=k∑​λk​∣ek​⟩⟨ek​∣ where ⟨ek​∣el​⟩=δkl​

  • Eigenvalues are genereally complex with a magnitude of 111. So, they can be written as eiθe^{i\theta}eiθ: (proof)
    ∣λk∣=1  ⟺  λk=eiθ|\lambda_k| = 1\quad\iff\quad\lambda_k=e^{i\theta}∣λk​∣=1⟺λk​=eiθ

  • Preserves inner product between any two states: (proof)
    (and as a result, also preserves norm of any state.)
    ∣ψ′⟩=U ⁣∣ψ⟩ and ∣ϕ′⟩=U ⁣∣ϕ⟩  ⟺  ⟨ψ′∣ϕ′⟩=⟨ψ∣ϕ⟩\ket{\psi'} = U\!\ket\psi\text{~and~}\ket{\phi'} = U\!\ket\phi\quad\iff\quad\braket{\psi'|\phi'}=\braket{\psi|\phi}∣ψ′⟩=U∣ψ⟩ and ∣ϕ′⟩=U∣ϕ⟩⟺⟨ψ′∣ϕ′⟩=⟨ψ∣ϕ⟩

Hermitian Operators (H†=H)\left(H^\dag =H\right)(H†=H)

Also known as self-adjoint operators.

  • HHH is also normal because H†H=H2=HH†H^\dag H = H^2 = H H^\dagH†H=H2=HH†. So spectral decomposition also applies here: (proof)
    H=∑kλk∣ek⟩ ⁣ ⁣⟨ek∣ where ⟨ek∣el⟩=δklH=\sum_k \lambda_k \ket{e_k}\!\!\bra{e_k} \text{~where~}\braket{e_k|e_l}=\delta_{kl}H=k∑​λk​∣ek​⟩⟨ek​∣ where ⟨ek​∣el​⟩=δkl​

  • Eigenvalues are real, λk∈R\lambda_k\in\mathbb Rλk​∈R. (proof)

  • For any state ∣ψ⟩\ket\psi∣ψ⟩ in a Hilbert space, we get, (proof)
    ⟨ψ∣H∣ψ⟩∈R\braket{\psi|H|\psi} \in\mathbb R⟨ψ∣H∣ψ⟩∈R

  • Related to a unitary UUU by the equation, U=eiHU=e^{iH}U=eiH. (proof)

Positive-Semidefinite Operators (S†=S⪰0)\left(S^\dag=S\succeq0\right)(S†=S⪰0)

Most authors also call them Positive operators. But some author define Positive operators to be strictly positive (S≻0)\left(S\succ0\right)(S≻0). However, in most physics context, it usually refers to Positive-semidefinite operator. And if anywhere we need the strict case, it is referred to as Positive-definite operator.

  • The defining characteristics of positive operator is that for any state ∣ψ⟩\ket\psi∣ψ⟩, we get, ⟨ψ∣S∣ψ⟩≥0\braket{\psi|S|\psi} \geq 0⟨ψ∣S∣ψ⟩≥0
  • In a Hilbert space, it is can be proven that the definition implies that SSS is also Hermitian, S†=SS^\dag = SS†=S. So no need to explicitly mention that in case of Hilbert space. However, in real vector spaces, this is not the case and Hermicity must be included in the definition.
    For example, the operator that rotates real vectors by 60°60\degree60° in R2\mathbb R^2R2, R(60°)R(60\degree)R(60°) on a real vector space, is not a positive operator because it’s not Hermitian. Even though, for any normalized state ∣ψ⟩\ket\psi∣ψ⟩, the inner product ⟨ψ∣R(60°)∣ψ⟩=cos⁡60°=12\braket{\psi|R(60^\degree)|\psi}=\cos60^\degree=\frac12⟨ψ∣R(60°)∣ψ⟩=cos60°=21​. So, for any state ∣ψ⟩\ket\psi∣ψ⟩, we have ⟨ψ∣R(60°)∣ψ⟩≥0\braket{\psi|R(60^\degree)|\psi}\geq0⟨ψ∣R(60°)∣ψ⟩≥0.
  • Eigenvalues are non-negative, λk≥0\lambda_k\geq0λk​≥0. And eigenvectors form orthonormal basis. Substituting ∣ψ⟩=∣ek⟩\ket\psi=\ket{e_k}∣ψ⟩=∣ek​⟩ in the definition provides an easy proof.
  • Has a unique Positive (and Hermitian) square root, S=R2S=R^2S=R2 where,
    R=∑kλk∣ek⟩ ⁣ ⁣⟨ek∣ where ⟨ek∣el⟩=δklR=\sum_k \sqrt{\lambda_k} \ket{e_k}\!\!\bra{e_k} \text{~where~}\braket{e_k|e_l}=\delta_{kl}R=k∑​λk​​∣ek​⟩⟨ek​∣ where ⟨ek​∣el​⟩=δkl​

Projection Operators (P2=P)\left(P^2 =P\right)(P2=P)

Some authors mean Orthogonal projection operator. But Projection operators are more general than that. For example, (1100)\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix}(10​10​) is an Oblique projection operator.

  • If PPP projects onto the subspace XXX, then for any ∣x⟩∈X\ket x\in X∣x⟩∈X,
    P ⁣∣x⟩=∣x⟩P\!\ket x = \ket xP∣x⟩=∣x⟩
    And for any state ∣y⟩∉X\ket y\notin X∣y⟩∈/X, we have P ⁣∣y⟩=0P\!\ket y=0P∣y⟩=0.
  • Eigenvalues of PPP are either 111 or 000:
    (But eigenvectors are not necessarily orthogonal. So, may not be a positive operator.)
    λk∈{0,1}\lambda_k\in\{0,1\}λk​∈{0,1}

Orthogonal Projection Operators (P2=P=P†)\left(P^2 =P=P^\dag\right)(P2=P=P†)

  • By definition, PPP is Hermitian (and by extension, also normal).
  • PPP is also positive because the eigenvalues are either 111 or 000:
    λk∈{0,1}\lambda_k\in\{0,1\}λk​∈{0,1}
  • Since PPP is a normal operator, spectral decomposition can be applied: (proof)
    P=∑k∣ek⟩ ⁣ ⁣⟨ek∣ where ⟨ek∣el⟩=δklP = \sum_k\ket{e_k}\!\!\bra{e_k} \text{~where~}\braket{e_k|e_l}=\delta_{kl}P=k∑​∣ek​⟩⟨ek​∣ where ⟨ek​∣el​⟩=δkl​
  • If P≠IP\neq IP=I, then P−1P^{-1}P−1 does not exist.

Puali Operators (X,Y,Z)(X,Y,Z)(X,Y,Z)

There are only 3 Pauli operators, {X,Y,Z}\{X,Y,Z\}{X,Y,Z}. These are the most important single qubit operators there is. Pauli operators have the following properties:

  • Unitary:
    X†=X−1,Y†=Y−1,Z†=Z−1X^\dag=X^{-1},\qquad Y^\dag=Y^{-1},\qquad Z^\dag=Z^{-1}X†=X−1,Y†=Y−1,Z†=Z−1
  • Hermitian:
    X†=X,Y†=Y,Z†=ZX^\dag=X,\qquad Y^\dag=Y,\qquad Z^\dag=ZX†=X,Y†=Y,Z†=Z
  • Involutory (inverse of itself, and so, squares to identity) since both unitary and Hermitian:
    X2=Y2=Z2=IX^2=Y^2=Z^2=IX2=Y2=Z2=I
  • The two eigenvalues are +1+1+1 and −1-1−1:
    eig⁡(X)=eig⁡(Y)=eig⁡(Z)=±1\def\eig{\operatorname{eig}}\eig(X)=\eig(Y)=\eig(Z)=\pm1eig(X)=eig(Y)=eig(Z)=±1
  • Traceless (since sum of eigenvalues is 000):
    tr⁡(X)=tr⁡(Y)=tr⁡(Z)=0\def\tr{\operatorname{tr}}\tr(X)=\tr(Y)=\tr(Z)=0tr(X)=tr(Y)=tr(Z)=0
  • Determinant is −1-1−1 (since product of eigenvalues is −1-1−1):
    det⁡(X)=det⁡(Y)=det⁡(Z)=−1\det(X)=\det(Y)=\det(Z)=-1det(X)=det(Y)=det(Z)=−1
  • Anti-commutes with other Pauli operators:
    XY=−YX,YZ=−ZY,ZX=−XZXY=-YX,\qquad YZ=-ZY,\qquad ZX=-XZXY=−YX,YZ=−ZY,ZX=−XZ
  • Relation between Pauli operators:
    XY=iZ,YZ=iX,ZX=iYXY=iZ,\qquad YZ=iX,\qquad ZX=iYXY=iZ,YZ=iX,ZX=iY

Pauli Group

By including III with Pauli operators and extending to nnn-qubits, we get Pauli group. In other words, P\mathcal PP is an operator in Pauli group iff:
P=ikP1⊗P2⊗…Pnwherek∈Z4, Pi∈{I,X,Y,Z}\mathcal P = i^kP_1\otimes P_2\otimes \dots P_n\quad\text{where}\quad k\in\Z_4,~P_i\in\{I,X,Y,Z\}P=ikP1​⊗P2​⊗…Pn​wherek∈Z4​, Pi​∈{I,X,Y,Z}

Clifford Group (CPC†=CPC−1=P′)\left(C\mathcal PC^\dag=C\mathcal PC^{-1}=\mathcal P'\right)(CPC†=CPC−1=P′)

Operators in Clifford group are unitary operators that map Pauli group to Pauli group by conjugation. For example, Hadamard operator HHH is a Clifford operator because HIH†=I, HXH†=Z, HZH†=X, HYH†=−Y, iI=XYZHIH^\dag=I,~HXH^\dag=Z,~HZH^\dag=X,~HYH^\dag=-Y,~iI=XYZHIH†=I, HXH†=Z, HZH†=X, HYH†=−Y, iI=XYZ

  • Pauli group is a subset of Clifford group. Because, by definition, a group maps onto itself.
  • Any Clifford operator can be made from the operators H,S,CXH,S,CXH,S,CX. Here, HHH is the Hadamard operator, SSS is the 90° ⁣90\degree\!90°-phase operator (i.e. S2=ZS^2=ZS2=Z) and CXCXCX is the controlled not operation. In other words, these are the generators of the Clifford group:
    C=⟨H,S,CX⟩\mathcal C = \big\langle H,S,CX \big\rangleC=⟨H,S,CX⟩
  • Gottesman-Knill theorem states that any Clifford circuit starting with computation basis states and being measured in computational basis, can be efficiently simulated using classical computers. This is specially useful in stabilizer code.
  • Universal quantum gate sets require TTT gate along side Clifford gates.

Theorem 1

Let A=M+M†2A=\frac{M+M^\dag}2A=2M+M†​ and B=M−M†2iB=\frac{M-M^\dag}{2i}B=2iM−M†​. Then,
A†=(M+M†2)†=M†+(M†)†2=M†+M2=A A^\dag = \left(\frac{M+M^\dag}2\right)^\dag = \frac{M^\dag+(M^\dag)^\dag}2 = \frac{M^\dag+M}2 = A A†=(2M+M†​)†=2M†+(M†)†​=2M†+M​=A

Theorem 5

Let NNN be a normal operator. Then its eigenvectors corresponding to different eigenvalues are orthogonal.
⟨e1∣e2⟩=0whereλ1≠λ2, N ⁣∣ek⟩=λk∣ek⟩\braket{e_1|e_2}=0 \text{\quad where\quad} \lambda_1\neq\lambda_2,~ N\!\ket{e_k}=\lambda_k\ket{e_k}⟨e1​∣e2​⟩=0whereλ1​=λ2​, N∣ek​⟩=λk​∣ek​⟩

Theorem 6 (Spectral Theorem)

Eigenvectors of a normal operator form an orthonormal basis. So a normal operator NNN can be written as:
N=∑kλk∣ek⟩ ⁣ ⁣⟨ek∣ where ⟨ek∣el⟩=δklN=\sum_k \lambda_k \ket{e_k}\!\!\bra{e_k} \text{~where~}\braket{e_k|e_l}=\delta_{kl}N=k∑​λk​∣ek​⟩⟨ek​∣ where ⟨ek​∣el​⟩=δkl​

Proof

Let’s split the theorem into two cases, the “non-degenerate” case (every eigenvalue is different) and the “degenerate” case (two or more eigenvalues are same).
Case I: Non-degenerate eigenspace
If any two eigenvalues of NNN are different from each other, we can use theorem 5 to show that all the eigenvectors are orthogonal to each other. If ∣ek⟩\ket{e_k}∣ek​⟩ and ∣el⟩\ket{e_l}∣el​⟩ are eigenvectors of NNN with corresponding eigenvalues λk\lambda_kλk​ and λk\lambda_kλk​ where λk≠λl\lambda_k\neq\lambda_lλk​=λl​,
⟨ek∣el⟩=0\braket{e_k|e_l}=0⟨ek​∣el​⟩=0
If k=lk=lk=l, since ∣ek⟩\ket{e_k}∣ek​⟩ is normalized, ⟨ek∣el⟩=1\braket{e_k|e_l}=1⟨ek​∣el​⟩=1. In other words, in the non-degenerate case, we have,
⟨ek∣el⟩=δkl\braket{e_k|e_l}=\delta_{kl}⟨ek​∣el​⟩=δkl​
Now it is easy to see that the statement, N=∑kλk∣ek⟩ ⁣ ⁣⟨ek∣N=\sum_k \lambda_k \ket{e_k}\!\!\bra{e_k}N=∑k​λk​∣ek​⟩⟨ek​∣ must be true because,
N ⁣∣el⟩=∑kλk∣ek⟩ ⁣ ⁣⟨ek∣el⟩=∑kδklλk∣ek⟩=λl∣el⟩N\!\ket{e_l}=\sum_k \lambda_k \ket{e_k}\!\!\braket{e_k|e_l} =\sum_k\delta_{kl} \lambda_k \ket{e_k} =\lambda_l\ket{e_l}N∣el​⟩=k∑​λk​∣ek​⟩⟨ek​∣el​⟩=k∑​δkl​λk​∣ek​⟩=λl​∣el​⟩
which is trivially true since ∣el⟩\ket{e_l}∣el​⟩ is an eigenvector of NNN with the eigenvalue of λl\lambda_lλl​.
Case II: Degenerate eigenspace
If we have same eigenvalue for some of the eigenvalues, then the corresponding eigenvectors need not be orthogonal. In fact we may have multiple degenerate eigenspace.
However, in each degenerate case, we can use Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization process to find a set of orthonormal eigenvectors where ⟨ek∣el⟩=δkl\braket{e_k|e_l}=\delta_{kl}⟨ek​∣el​⟩=δkl​.
For the case of eigenspaces with different eigenvalues, by theorem 5, it is guaranteed that such eigenspaces are orthogonal to each other.
Thus we can find a complete orthonormal basis for the complex vector space. And each of this statevectors are eigenvectors of the operator NNN. So, following similar steps to the non-degenerate case, we can write,
N=∑kλk∣ek⟩ ⁣ ⁣⟨ek∣ where ⟨ek∣el⟩=δklN=\sum_k \lambda_k \ket{e_k}\!\!\bra{e_k} \text{~where~}\braket{e_k|e_l}=\delta_{kl}N=k∑​λk​∣ek​⟩⟨ek​∣ where ⟨ek​∣el​⟩=δkl​

Theorem 10

⟨e∣H∣e⟩=⟨e∣λ∣e⟩=λ⟨e∣e⟩=λ\braket{e|H|e} = \braket{e|\lambda|e}=\lambda\braket{e|e}=\lambda⟨e∣H∣e⟩=⟨e∣λ∣e⟩=λ⟨e∣e⟩=λ
